Tribute to Satyajit Ray

Bengal's Renaissance Man

    Here's a timeline of Satyajit Ray's life:

  • 1921 - Born in Calcutta, India.
  • 1940 - Attends Visva-Bharati University at Santiniketan, founded by Rabindranath Tagore.
  • 1947 - Founded the Calcutta Film Society.
  • 1949 - Ray married Bijoya Das, his first cousin and long-time sweetheart. They have a son, Sandip, who is also a film director.
  • 1950 - Ray met Jean Renoir while The River was in production, and the two men became friends. Ray met Subrata Mitra, a production assistant on this film and Ray's eventual cinematographer, during filming.
  • 1952 - Ray started shooting for Pather Panchali (Song of the Road), based on the novel of the same name by Bibhutibhushan Bandyopadhyay, with an inexperienced crew, consisting of cameraman Subrata Mitra and art director Bansi Chandragupta, who went on to achieve great acclaim.
  • 1955 - Pather Panchali was released to great critical and popular success. After borrowing a loan from the West Bengal government, Ray managed to complete the film. It earned numerous prizes and had long runs in both India and abroad. This was the first of the Apu trilogy.
  • 1956 - Aparajito (The Unvanquished) was released. The second of the Apu trilogy
  • 1959 - The last of the trilogy, Apur Sansar (The World of Apu), was released. In this film, Ray introduced two of his favourite actors, Soumitra Chatterjee and Sharmila Tagore.
  • 1962 - Kanchenjungha, based on his first original screenplay, his first film in colour, was released. This was one of the earliest examples of a narrative structure, which later came to be known as hyperlink film.
  • 1964 - Charulata (The Lonely Wife), based on "Nastanirh" (The Broken Nest), a short story of Tagore, the film tells of a lonely wife, Charu, in 19th-century Bengal, and her growing feelings for her brother-in-law Amal, was released. Many critics as his most accomplished film.
  • 1966 - Nayak (The Hero), the story of a screen hero travelling in a train and meeting a young, sympathetic female journalist, was released. Starring legendary Bengali film actor Uttam Kumar, the film explores the inner conflict of the apparently highly successful matin√©e idol, in the twenty-four hours of the journey.
  • 1970 - The first film of Ray's Calcutta Trilogy was released. Pratidwandi (The Adversary) is about an idealist young graduate; if disillusioned at the end of film, he is still uncorrupted.
  • 1971 - The second film of Ray's Calcutta Trilogy was released. Jana Aranya (The Middleman) showed a young man giving in to the culture of corruption to make a living.
  • 1975 - The third film of Ray's Calcutta Trilogy was released. Seemabaddha (Company Limited) portrayed an already successful man giving up his morality for further gains.
  • 1977 - Ray completed and released Shatranj Ke Khilari (The Chess Players), a Hindi/Urdu film based on a short story by Munshi Premchand. It was set in Lucknow in the state of Oudh, a year before the Indian rebellion of 1857. A commentary on issues related to the colonisation of India by the British, this was Ray's first feature film in a language other than Bengali.
  • 1983 - While working on Ghare Baire (Home and the World), Ray suffered a heart attack; it would severely limit his productivity in the remaining 9 years of his life. The film was released in 1984
  • 1991 - Ray's last film, Agantuk (The Stranger), was released.This film is lighter in mood but not in theme. When a long-lost uncle arrives to visit his niece in Calcutta, he arouses suspicion as to his motive. This provokes far-ranging questions in the film about civilisation.
  • 1992 - Ray's health deteriorated due to heart complications. He was admitted to a hospital, but never recovered. The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences awarded him an Honorary Academy Award. Ray is the first and the only Indian, yet, to receive the honor. Twenty-four days before his death, Ray accepted the award in a gravely ill condition, calling it the "Best achievement of [his] movie-making career." He died on 23 April 1992.
